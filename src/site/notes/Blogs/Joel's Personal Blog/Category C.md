Someone had told him he must move on, and he accepted this, though he did not understand what it meant or where precisely he was expected to go.

He consulted the appropriate authorities. The first said: time heals. The second said: you must simply decide. The third opened a drawer, withdrew a form, and asked him to specify the nature of his attachment - whether it was Category A (romantic), Category B (habitual), or Category C (unclassifiable). He stared at the form for a long time. He wrote Category C. The third authority stamped it without reading it and told him to return in six to eight weeks.

He returned. There was no record of his previous visit.

The strange thing was not that he loved her. The strange thing was that the loving continued to operate entirely without his permission - like a bureau that processes applications long after the government that created it has dissolved. He would wake each morning and check, methodically, whether it had stopped. It had not stopped. He noted this in a small book he kept beside his bed. The entries were identical. Eventually he stopped writing the date.

His neighbours, who were well-meaning, came to him on a Tuesday.

“You must move on,” they said, in unison, which he found unusual but did not remark upon.

“Yes,” he said. “I have been told this.”

“Are you moving on?” they asked.

“I am attempting to locate the correct procedure,” he said.

They nodded slowly, as though this explained something that had long troubled them, and left.

He imagined - and this was the part that undid him most thoroughly - that somewhere there existed a bureau specifically designed for this purpose. The Bureau for the Relinquishment of Persistent Affections, perhaps. That there were clerks there, grey and patient, who handled such cases daily. That one could go to them with the necessary documents - proof of loving, proof of loss, proof of the hope that refused, despite everything, to file its own resignation -and that they would process it all, stamp it all, and hand back across the counter a new version of himself: lighter, re-wired, indifferent in the specific way that passing time is said to make one indifferent.

He would have walked there immediately. He would have waited in whatever queue they kept, however long, however cold the corridor.

But he could not find the bureau. He looked on several maps. He asked at the post office. The man at the post office said he had never heard of it but wrote down an address anyway, because it seemed unkind not to. The address led to a laundry. The woman at the laundry said she had been asked this before.

The hope was the worst administrative problem.

Grief, he had come to understand, is a closed file. It has an end date. One stamps it, archives it, and though the archive is always there - always retrievable, always heavier than you remember - it is, at least, closed.

But hope has no end date. Hope is the document that keeps resubmitting itself. He would reject it, and it would return, slightly reformatted, with a new cover letter, patient and without embarrassment, as though the previous rejection had simply been lost in the post.

He did not know how to refuse it permanently. There was no form for that either.

At night he lay very still and tried to reason with himself.

“You must move on,” he told himself.

“Yes,” he agreed.

“Will you?”

A long silence followed during which he listened to the building settle around him - the pipes, the walls, the slow and indifferent respiration of a structure that had housed many tenants and remembered none of them.

“My conscience,” he said finally, to no one, “does not permit it.”

This struck him as both deeply true and entirely useless, which, he had come to understand, was the condition of most true things.

He closed his eyes. The loving continued its paperwork. The hope resubmitted its application.

Somewhere, in a bureau he could not find, a clerk stamped a form with today's date.

The form said: Pending.

It had always said: Pending.

He got up in the morning. He made coffee. He carried the file with him, as he always did - not because he had chosen to, but because no one had yet told him where to put it down, and he had not, in all his searching, found a place that would take it.
