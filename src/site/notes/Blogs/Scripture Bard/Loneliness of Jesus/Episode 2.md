# No Place to Lay His Head—And No One to Follow Him There
> The following conversation portrayed in this reflection is not a verbatim account found in the biblical text but has been imaginatively constructed for the purpose of exploring the theme of Jesus’ loneliness and the cost of discipleship. While rooted in the Gospel material where Jesus says “Foxes have dens and birds have nests, but the Son of Man has nowhere to lay his head” (cf. Matthew 8:18–20; Luke 9:57–58), the dialogue itself is a creative engagement with that scene to aid reflection.

The words came out strong and confident. The man has been watching from the edges of the crowd for weeks now. He’s seen the miracles. He has heard the teachings that cut through religious pretense. He has watched the way Jesus looks at people. And now, finally, he’s ready. Ready to step forward. Ready to commit himself. Ready to be part of whatever movement this is becoming.

Jesus stopped walking. He turned to him slowly, and there’s something in his eyes the man didn’t expect. There was no gratitude. No excitement. It was something closer to weariness. “Wherever I go?” Jesus asked. “Yes,” the man said, nodding. “Wherever. I mean it. I’ve been thinking about this for weeks. I want to learn from you. I want to be part of this. I’m ready.”

Jesus studied him for a long moment. Then he asks, “Where did you sleep last night?” The question catches the man off guard. “I—what?” “Where did you sleep?” Jesus asks again. “Last night. Where did you lay your head?”

“At home,” the man says, confused. “In my bed. Why?”

“And the night before that?”

“The same place. Teacher, I don’t understand—”

“And tonight?” Jesus interrupted him gently. “Where will you sleep tonight?”

The man hesitates. “I… I suppose that depends. If I’m following you, then wherever you—” He stops. Looks at Jesus more carefully. “Where will you sleep tonight?”

Jesus doesn’t answer immediately. Instead, he gestures to the hillside around them, to the path stretching ahead, to the distant town where smoke rises from evening fires. “Do you see those foxes?” he asks, pointing to a burrow half-hidden in the rocks. “They have holes. Dark places underground. Safe places. When the world becomes too much, they retreat there. They have somewhere to go.”

The man nods slowly, still not understanding where this is going. “And the birds,” Jesus continues, looking up at the branches above them where sparrows are settling in for the evening. “They have nests. Woven refuges. Places they’ve built, places they return to every night. They know where they belong.” He turns back to the man, and his voice drops. “But the Son of Man? I have nowhere to lay my head.”

The words hang in the air between them. The man opens his mouth, then closes it. Opens it again. “You mean—you don’t have a house?”

“I don’t have a house,” Jesus confirms.

“But surely someone… I mean, your followers, they must… Where did you sleep last night?”

“On the boat,” Jesus says. “The one Simon Peter’s family owns. They let me sleep there sometimes. When they’re not using it. When they remember I might need it.”

“And the night before that?”

“In Martha’s home. In Bethany. She’s kind. She makes space for me when I’m passing through.” Jesus pauses. “But I was passing through. I’m always passing through.”

The man feels something in his chest. This isn’t the conversation he expected to have. “But you could—I mean, surely with everything you’re doing, people would—”

“People would what?” Jesus asks. There’s no edge in his voice, only genuine curiosity. “Offer me a room? They do, sometimes. For a night. Maybe two. But then life continues. Their families need the space. Their routines return. And I move on because I was never meant to stay. Do you understand what I’m telling you?”

“I think so,” the man says, though he’s not sure he does.

“I don’t think you do,” Jesus says gently. “You said you would follow me wherever I go. But I don’t go anywhere that stays. There is no ‘wherever’ that becomes ‘here.’ There is only the road. Only the next town. Only the borrowed rooms and the temporary arrangements and the constant awareness that nothing is mine.”

The man feels his certainty beginning to crack. “But that’s—that’s temporary, isn’t it? I mean, once your ministry is established, once you have more followers, surely you’ll—”

“Settle down?” Jesus finishes. “Build something permanent? Find a home base?” He shakes his head. “This is not temporary. This is the life. This is what it means to be the Son of Man. And if you follow me, it will be your life too.”

Silence falls between them. The man can hear his own breathing, louder than it should be. Finally, he asks, “Why are you telling me this?”

“Because you said ‘wherever,’” Jesus replies. “And I need you to understand what ‘wherever’ means. I need you to count the cost before you say yes. Because if you don’t count it now, you’ll resent it later. And I’d rather you walk away honest than follow me bitter.”

The man’s hands clench at his sides. “I’m not going to walk away. I told you, I’m ready. I’ve thought about this.”

“Have you?” Jesus asks. “Have you thought about what it means to never have a door with your name on it? To never have a bed that’s always there? To depend on other people’s generosity, which can be withdrawn at any moment? To watch everyone else go home while you wonder where you’ll sleep?”

“People would make space—”

“For how long?” Jesus interrupts, and there’s something raw in his voice now. “A week? A month? How long before you become a burden? How long before the welcome becomes thin? How long before you see it in their eyes—the calculation, the weariness, the wish that you would just move on already?”

The man stares at him. This is not the Jesus he’s been watching from the crowd. This is someone more vulnerable. More exposed. More… lonely.

“You think this is about adventure,” Jesus continues. “About purpose. About being part of something important. And it is those things. But it’s also about exhaustion. About the weight of never landing. About the loneliness of always being a guest in other people’s spaces.” He pauses. “Do you know what the hardest part is?”

The man shakes his head.

“It’s not the physical discomfort,” Jesus says. “It’s not the uncertainty of where I’ll sleep or what I’ll eat. It’s the knowledge that everyone around me gets to rest in ways I don’t. They have homes to return to. Families who claim them. Spaces where they can stop performing, stop teaching, stop being ‘on.’ But me?” He gestures to himself. “I am always on. Always moving. Always dependent. Always aware that I have nowhere to lay my head.”

“But you have followers,” the man protests. “You have the twelve. You have—”

“I have people who follow me during the day,” Jesus says quietly. “But at night? They go home. Even the twelve—they have families, connections, places they belong. I am teaching them to leave those things behind, yes. But they haven’t fully left them yet. And when they do, when they finally understand what I’m asking…” He trails off, looking toward Jerusalem in the distance. “They’ll scatter. When it gets hard enough, when the cost becomes real enough, they’ll go back to their boats and their tax tables and their homes.”

“You don’t know that,” the man says, but his voice lacks conviction.

“Don’t I?” Jesus asks. He looks at the man directly. “You came to me with enthusiasm. With certainty. And now? I can see it in your eyes. You’re already calculating. Already wondering if this is what you really want. Already feeling the pull back toward your bed, your routine, your life that makes sense.”

The man’s throat tightens. “I’m still here, aren’t I?”

“For now,” Jesus says. “But the question isn’t whether you’re here now. The question is whether you’ll be here tomorrow. Next week. Next month. When the excitement fades and the reality sets in and you realize that following me means giving up everything you thought would make you secure.”

“I can do that,” the man insists, though he’s no longer sure.

Jesus’s expression softens. “Can you? Or do you just think you should be able to? There’s a difference.” He sits down on a nearby stone, suddenly looking very tired. “Let me ask you something. When you imagine following me, what do you see?”

The man sits down too, grateful for the pause. “I see… purpose. Meaning. Being part of changing the world.”

“And where do you sleep in this vision?” Jesus asks.

The man opens his mouth, then closes it. He hadn’t thought about that part.

“Exactly,” Jesus says. “Because we don’t imagine the lonely parts, do we? We imagine the glory. The significance. The moments that matter. We don’t imagine the nights when you can’t sleep because the ground is too hard and you’re too cold and you’re too aware that you have nowhere else to go. We don’t imagine the mornings when you wake up exhausted and realize you have to do it all over again.”

“But it’s worth it, isn’t it?” the man asks. “For the kingdom? For what you’re building?”

“Is it?” Jesus asks back to test him. “I’m not asking rhetorically. I’m genuinely asking. Is it worth it to you? Not in theory. In practice. Is the kingdom worth sleeping on boats? Worth depending on strangers? Worth the loneliness of never truly belonging anywhere?”

The man struggles to answer. Finally, he says, “It should be.”

“‘Should be’ isn’t the same as ‘is,’” Jesus observes. “And I’m not interested in ‘should.’ I’m interested in truth. In reality. In whether you can actually live this life or whether you’ll resent it and resent me for leading you into it.”

“Why does it have to be this hard?” the man asks, and he hears the frustration in his own voice. “Why can’t you just… have a home? Build something stable? Let people come to you instead of always going to them?”

Jesus looks at him with something like compassion. “Because the kingdom I’m bringing doesn’t come with buildings and boundaries. It doesn’t come with addresses and ownership. It comes with movement and risk and the constant unsettling of everything people think they know about God.” He pauses. “And it comes with homelessness. Not as a bug, but as a feature. Because you can’t build the kingdom of God on the same foundations as the kingdoms of this world.”

“So you’re choosing this?” the man asks. “The homelessness? The loneliness? You’re choosing it?”

“I’m accepting it,” Jesus corrects. “There’s a difference. I don’t love being homeless. I don’t romanticize the loneliness. But I’ve accepted that this is the cost of the calling. And I won’t compromise the calling to ease the cost.”

The man feels something twisting inside him. Admiration and resistance, all tangled together. “But don’t you wish…” He stops, unsure how to finish.

“Don’t I wish what?” Jesus prompts gently.

“Don’t you wish someone would share it with you? The homelessness. The loneliness. Don’t you wish you didn’t have to carry it alone?”

Jesus is quiet for a long moment. When he speaks, his voice is barely above a whisper. “Every single day.”

The vulnerability in those words hits the man like a physical blow. “Then why—”

“Because wishing doesn’t change reality,” Jesus says. “I wish the road were easier. I wish more people could walk it with me. I wish the cost were lower and the companionship more certain. But wishes don’t build the kingdom. Faithfulness does. And faithfulness, right now, looks like walking this road even when I walk it mostly alone.”

“Mostly alone,” the man repeats. “Not completely alone?”

“There are people who stay,” Jesus acknowledges. “For a while. For longer than I expect sometimes. But even they…” He shakes his head. “Even they go home eventually. Even they reach a point where the cost is too high. And I don’t blame them. How could I? I’m asking them to give up everything. Everything. Security, comfort, certainty, home. Who can blame anyone for choosing not to pay that price?”

“But you’re paying it,” the man says.

“Because I have to,” Jesus replies. “Because this is my calling. My road. My cross to carry.” He looks at the man intently. “That’s what I’m trying to tell you. You could probably admire this from a distance. You could possibly support it. You can choose to believe in it. But you could in your wisdom, choose not to live it..”

The man feels tears prickling at the corners of his eyes, though he’s not sure why. “You make it sound like you want me to leave.”

“I don’t want you to leave,” Jesus says. “But I don’t want you to stay for the wrong reasons either. I don’t want you to stay because you think you should, or because you’re ashamed not to, or because you can’t bear to disappoint me. I want you to stay only if you can bear the cost. Only if you can live with the homelessness and the loneliness and the constant uncertainty.” He pauses. “Can you?”

The man looks down at his hands. They’re shaking slightly. He thinks about his bed. His house. His life that makes sense. He thinks about his family, his routine, his certainties. And then he thinks about Jesus, alone on a boat, borrowing space, never landing.

“I don’t think I can,” he whispers.

Jesus nods slowly. “I know.”

“I’m sorry,” the man says, and he means it. “I really thought—I wanted to—”

“I know,” Jesus says again. “You came with an honest heart. You’re leaving with an honest heart. That’s more than most people manage.”

The man stands up slowly, feeling like he’s aged years in the last few minutes. “What will you do now?”

Jesus stands too. “What I always do. Keep walking. Keep teaching. Keep looking for the few who can bear the weight.” He manages a small smile. “And keep hoping that tonight, someone will offer me a place to lay my head.”

“They will,” the man says. “Someone will. You’re—people love you.”

“People love what I can do for them,” Jesus corrects gently. “But loving me and sheltering me are different things. One costs nothing. The other costs everything.” He places a hand on the man’s shoulder. “Go home. Love your family. Live your life. And when you tell people about this conversation—and you will tell people—tell them the truth. Tell them I understood you. Tell them I didn’t blame you for leaving.”

“But you’re disappointed,” the man says.

Jesus considers this. “I’m lonely,” he finally says. “But I was lonely before you came. And I’ll be lonely after you leave. That’s not your fault. That’s just the shape of the road I’m walking.”

The man wants to say something profound, something that will make this hurt less. But there’s nothing to say. So he just nods, turns, and starts walking back toward town. Back toward his bed. His certainty. His home.

He doesn’t look back. If he looks back, he might see Jesus still standing there, watching him go. And he’s not sure he could bear that.

Behind him, Jesus does stand there. Watching. Not with anger or resentment, but with a deep, aching painful acceptance. This is not the first time someone has walked away. It won’t be the last. He’s learned to absorb the leaving. To carry the loneliness of having nowhere to lay his head and no one willing to share that placelessness with him.

He stands there until the man disappears around the bend. Then he picks up his worn traveling bag—the only possession he owns—and continues down the road. Toward the next town. The next borrowed room. The next night of uncertainty.

Somewhere ahead, Jerusalem waits. And a cross. And a loneliness so complete that even heaven will seem distant. But that’s still to come. For now, there’s just the road. And the fading light. And the weight of knowing that when night falls, he’ll need to ask someone—again—if they have room for him. If they can spare a corner. If they wouldn’t mind hosting the homeless rabbi one more night.

And they’ll say yes, probably. They usually do. For one night.

**But one night is never enough to stop being lonely.**

He keeps walking anyway.

Because some roads must be walked even when they’re walked alone. Some truths must be carried even when no one helps carry them. Some callings cost everything, including companionship. **And as the sun sets behind him, Jesus asks himself the question he asks every night: Will anyone ever follow me all the way? Will anyone ever be willing to share this homelessness with me? Will I ever have someone who doesn’t eventually go home?**

The answer, he knows, is no. But he keeps asking anyway. Keeps hoping anyway. Keeps calling people anyway. **Because hope, like loneliness, is something you carry even when carrying it costs you everything**
