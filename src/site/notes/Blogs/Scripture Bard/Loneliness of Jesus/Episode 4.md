# Between the Crowd and the Cross: Jesus’ Lonely Middle Spaces

![This may contain: a person in a field with their arms outstretched](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3Dke!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6207d45b-6f72-49b8-98c7-15283e197bc7_734x473.jpeg "This may contain: a person in a field with their arms outstretched")

By now we have understood that Jesus was lonely in many respects. Not that he lacked a crowd, but that he was always misunderstood. They wanted Jesus for their self-interest and not for the right reasons. This hurts, doesn’t it? When people follow you for the wrong reasons. When they come for what you can give them, not for who you are. When they need your gifts but don’t want your heart.

I think about Jesus surrounded by thousands, yet completely alone. And my heart breaks a little. Because I know that feeling. Maybe you do too.

His loneliness is now getting intensified because, after a few days, he is going to take up the burden of all the people - including those who pained him, ridiculed him, and mocked him. He’s going to the cross, and die for them. Can you imagine? You’re already hurting from how people treated you. And now you’re walking toward something that will hurt even more. And you’re doing it for the very people who hurt you in the first place.

This is the _middle space loneliness_. And it’s definitely something painful. You are already in pain, and you are going to a more painful situation now.

I remember my own middle space, where I was misunderstood. I meant something else, but I was understood different. No explanations would convince. The more I tried to clarify, the more tangled it became. Words failed me. Intentions didn’t matter. I felt stuck between two worlds. The old one had already wounded me. The new one scared me. And I stood in the middle, not belonging anywhere. Not fitting in the past anymore. Not yet home in the future. Just...alone. Waiting. Wondering if anyone understood. I was in that “should I do or not do” scenario. Paralyzed between moving forward and staying still. Every option felt wrong and every choice felt heavy.

And in that lonely middle space, I met Jesus in a way I never had before.

**How do we handle the middle spaces?** The middle spaces, as the name suggests, is the middle. It’s between the past and the future. It’s the present. And the present is tough. It’s tough because you can’t go back, and you can’t rush forward. You just have to be here. In the middle.

The crowd cheers you, but not always. Crowds are temporary. They stay when you meet their needs. They stay when you do what they expect. But the moment you can’t heal their specific sickness, or answer their particular question, or give them what they came for - they leave. They go after their needs are met. They disappear if you did not work according to their expectations. And suddenly, you’re alone again.

Jesus knew this. One day, five thousand people followed him because he gave them bread. The next day, when he asked them to follow him to the cross, most of them walked away. Even his closest friends questioned whether they should stay.

**Where do you run in the middle space of your life?** Because you will have middle spaces. We all do. Times when you’re between jobs. Between callings. Between seasons. Between the old you and the new you. Between what you lost and what you hope for. Between the crowd’s approval and God’s silence.

Jesus ran to the Father, both after the crowd and before the crowd. Early in the morning, before anyone needed him, he went away to pray. Late at night, after everyone had taken from him, he went to the mountain to be with his Father. **Before the noise. After the noise. In the middle of the noise. He always ran to the same place.** To the Father.

**The Father never disappoints our middles.** He constantly refreshes our soul. Not by taking away the middle space, but by meeting us there. By sitting with us in the waiting. By speaking when everyone else is silent. By staying when the crowd has gone. **He makes sure we are empowered enough to handle the middles.** Not that we won’t feel lonely. We will. Not that it won’t hurt. It does. But somehow, there’s a strength we didn’t have before. A peace that doesn’t make sense. A quiet knowing that we’re not forgotten, even when we feel invisible.

I learned this in my middle space. When the crowds thinned and the noise died down, and it was just me and God - that’s when I heard his voice most clearly. That’s when I felt his presence most deeply. That’s when I knew him, **not as the God who performs miracles for crowds, but as the Father who stays with his lonely child.**

**The moment you are out of the middle, it’s crowd, public, debate, arguments, performance. Everyone has an opinion. Everyone has expectations. Everyone wants something. And you have to show up, speak up, give out.** There’s nothing wrong with this. Jesus did it too. But he never let the crowd replace the Father. He never let public ministry become a substitute for private intimacy.

**Do not run away from the middle space loneliness.** I know it hurts. I know you want to escape it. I know you want to fill it with noise or busyness or even more ministry. **But what if the middle space is actually a gift?** What if it’s the place where God strips away our need for the crowd’s approval? What if it’s where we learn to hear his voice above all other voices? What if it’s where we discover that being known by him is better than being needed by everyone else?

Jesus’ loneliest middle space was probably Gethsemane. The crowd was gone. The disciples fell asleep. The cross was coming. And he was alone with the Father, wrestling, weeping, & surrendering. He didn’t get answers to all his questions. He didn’t get relief from all his pain. **But he got the Father. And the Father was enough.**

That’s what I’m learning. In my middle spaces, I don’t always get clarity. I don’t always get quick solutions. I don’t always understand why things have to hurt so much. But I get the Father. And slowly, painfully, and beautifully - I’m learning that he is enough.

Between the crowd and the cross, Jesus was lonely. But he was not alone. The Father was with him. In the early morning hours. On the mountainside. In the garden. In the darkest middle space. The Father was there. And he’s with you too. In your middle space. In your loneliness. In your waiting. In that uncomfortable place between what was and what will be. He sees you. He knows you. He’s not disappointed in you for being there. He’s not rushing you through it. He’s just...with you.

**So maybe today, instead of running from the middle space, we run to the Father in the middle space. We let him meet us there. We let him refresh us there. We let him love us there.** Not because the loneliness will instantly disappear. But because we’ll discover that even in our loneliest moments, we are deeply, completely, forever loved.

**The middle space is real. The loneliness is real. But so is the Father’s presence.**
