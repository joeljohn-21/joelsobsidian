# The Strange Loneliness of Jesus’ Withdrawals
There’s a moment in Luke 5, which doesn’t make sense. Jesus has just healed a leper. Word spread like wildfire. Crowds gathered in large numbers. This is the moment every leader dreams of. Moment of maximum influence. Moment of peak visibility. The movement is growing. And then Jesus, just walks away. Not to another town. Not to a larger venue. Luke says, he withdrew to desolate places to pray. He ghosted his own revival.

This happens over and over again in the gospels. After miracles, He just disappears. After breakthroughs, he retreats back. **Success doesn’t energize him toward more activity – it drives him toward solitude.** The original language (Greek) reveals something even more striking, i.e his walking away into the intentional solitude wasn’t occasional. The verb tense suggests that it was his habit, his rhythm, and his pattern. Withdrawing to lonely places wasn’t Jesus’ emergency plan when things got overwhelming. It was his normal. **Jesus was just regularly, deliberately, unreachable.**
## **The Loneliness Nobody Wanted**

Here’s what makes this strange: Jesus isn’t lonely because he’s unwanted. **He’s lonely because he chooses to be.** This isn’t the loneliness of the kid, who is picked last for playing the game. This isn’t the loneliness of social rejection or failure or not fitting in. Jesus could have had crowds 24/7. He could have been surrounded, celebrated, needed every moment of every day. **Instead, he walked away from it all to be alone.**

That kind of loneliness is harder for us to understand than the kind that happens to us. We know what it’s like to be left out, overlooked, and ghosted. We know involuntary loneliness. But chosen loneliness? Strategic isolation? Loneliness as a spiritual discipline? Seriously? That feels like almost offensive.

And yet Jesus treats solitude like it’s essential, not exceptional. Like it’s part of the mission, not a break from it. Why?
## **When Solitude becomes survival**

I think Jesus withdrew because he knew something we’re still learning, i.e we cannot live faithfully on other people’s terms. The crowds wanted a miracle worker. The disciples wanted a political revolutionary. The religious leaders wanted him to play by their rules. Everyone had an agenda. Everyone had expectations. Everyone knew exactly what Jesus should do next. And if Jesus had tried to meet all those expectations, he would have lost himself completely.

**The wilderness gave him something the crowds couldn’t, i.e clarity about who he actually was.**

Not Jesus the Healer (though he healed).  
  
Not Jesus the Teacher (though he taught).  
  
Not Jesus the Miracle Worker (though he performed miracles).  
  
Just Jesus. The Son. The Beloved. The One sent by the Father.

In the noise of the crowd, those identities blur. Everyone’s voice starts to sound equally important. Every need feels equally urgent. And slowly, you start performing someone else’s script instead of living your own calling. **But in the wilderness, there’s only one voice. And for Jesus, that one voice mattered more than the roar of a thousand.**
## **The Loneliness we are living**

Here’s where this gets uncomfortably personal. How much of your exhaustion comes from trying to be who everyone else needs you to be? **How many versions of yourself are you maintaining right now?** The capable one at work. The patient one at home. The spiritual one at church. The fun one with friends. The strong one for your family. The productive one for your boss. The available one for everyone.

And when was the last time you were just… you? Not performing. Not managing expectations. Not monitoring how you’re being perceived. Just present with your actual self, your actual thoughts, your actual limits.

For most of us, that only happens when we’re alone. And we hate being alone. We scroll, we stream, we text, we fill every quiet moment with noise because silence feels dangerous. Loneliness feels like failure.

But what if loneliness isn’t always the problem? What if sometimes it’s the solution? Jesus withdrew not to escape people but to escape people’s expectations. Not to avoid ministry but to remember what ministry actually was. Not to be less engaged but to be more genuinely present when he returned. His loneliness wasn’t about hating the crowd. It was about loving the Father more.
## **The Withdrawals we are afraid to make**

There’s a loneliness you’re probably avoiding right now. Maybe it’s the loneliness of disappointing someone by saying no. The loneliness of stepping back from a role you’ve outgrown. The loneliness of setting a boundary people won’t understand. The loneliness of choosing rest in a culture that worships productivity. Maybe it’s the loneliness of being honest about your limits instead of pretending you’re limitless. The loneliness of letting someone be disappointed instead of destroying yourself to fix their problem. The loneliness of not being everything to everyone.

That kind of loneliness feels like failure. Like selfishness. Like you’re letting people down.

But Jesus modeled it as faithfulness. He walked away from people who needed him – legitimately needed him – because staying would have meant ministering out of depletion instead of fullness. **He withdrew from success because success on the crowd’s terms would have been failure on the Father’s terms.**

**And if Jesus needed that kind of withdrawal, what makes us think we don’t?**
## **The Progression we didn’t see coming**

Early in the Gospels, Jesus withdraws on purpose. But by the end, he’s alone against his will.  
  
The disciples who followed him everywhere? They fall asleep in Gethsemane when he begs them to stay awake.  
  
The crowds who chased him? They shout “Crucify him.”  
  
Peter who swore loyalty? He denies knowing Jesus three times.  
  
Judas who shared meals with him? He betrays him with a kiss.

The loneliness of the wilderness becomes the loneliness of the cross. Chosen solitude becomes forced abandonment. And on the cross, Jesus experiences the most devastating loneliness imaginable: “My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?” But here’s the thing – **I don’t think the cross would have been survivable without the wilderness.**

Jesus didn’t learn to be alone in Gethsemane. He’d been practicing for years. Every early morning on the mountain. Every late night in prayer. Every time he slipped away from the crowds and the disciples and the demands. He was learning to find his identity in the Father’s presence, not in human approval. And when human approval vanished completely, when every friend fled and even the Father turned his face away, Jesus had already built a foundation that abandonment couldn’t destroy.

**The chosen loneliness prepared him for the unchosen loneliness.**
## **What Your Loneliness Might Be Teaching You**

If you’re lonely right now, I can’t tell you why. Maybe it’s loneliness that needs healing – the loneliness of genuine rejection, of trauma, of isolation that comes from pain or sin or circumstances beyond your control. That loneliness is real and it matters and it’s not something to spiritualize away.

But maybe it’s the other kind. The kind Jesus knew in the wilderness. Maybe you’re lonely because you finally said no. Because you stopped performing. Because you set a boundary. Because you’re trying to hear God’s voice instead of managing everyone else’s expectations.

Maybe you’re lonely because you’re doing something right. The culture will tell you that’s wrong. It will tell you that, connection is everything. It will tell, isolation is the enemy. That you should be networking, engaging, building your platform, staying relevant, always accessible.

**But Jesus shows us a different way.** **He shows us that sometimes the most faithful thing you can do is become unreachable.** Not forever. Not as a permanent retreat. But for the time it takes to remember who you are when nobody’s watching. For the space you need to hear the voice that matters most.

## **Holy Loneliness**

Here’s what I’m learning: **loneliness isn’t the opposite of faith. Sometimes it’s the cost of it.** Faithfulness to God will sometimes create distance from people. Not because you love them less, but because you love God more. Not because you’re antisocial, but because you refuse to let the crowd define you. The wilderness strips away the applause. It removes the validation. It takes away the sense that you’re needed and important and making a difference.

And in that stripping away, you discover what’s left.  
  
If your identity is built on being needed, the wilderness will destroy you.  
  
If your worth comes from productivity, solitude will feel like death.  
  
If your sense of purpose depends on visible impact, obscurity will terrify you.

**But if your identity is “child of God,” the wilderness becomes the place you remember who you are.** And you realize that’s enough. Not because you don’t need people – you do. Not because community doesn’t matter – it does. But because before you are anything else to anyone else, you are known and loved and called by God. The wilderness teaches you that.

## **The Presence in the Absence**

Here’s the final truth, the one that holds all of this together: **Jesus’ loneliness means you’re never alone in yours.** When you withdraw to that quiet place, when you step back from the noise and the demands and the exhausting work of being everything to everyone – the One who withdrew to desolate places is with you.

When you’re abandoned against your will, when friends fail you and people leave and you face a loneliness you never chose – **the One who was forsaken on the cross knows exactly what that costs.** **Your loneliness, whether chosen or forced, is never loneliness he doesn’t understand.**

He’s been in the wilderness. He’s been in the garden. He’s been on the cross. And because he has, your loneliness can become something sacred. Not easy. Not comfortable. But purposeful. The loneliness of faithfulness is real. It hurts. **But it’s also where you learn that God’s presence is enough even when everyone else’s presence is gone.**

**Jesus was alone on purpose. And sometimes, so must we be.**

Not because we hate people. But because we love God more.  
  
Not because we’re running away. But because we’re running toward the voice that knows our name. In the wilderness, you discover you’re not who the crowd says you are. You’re who God says you are.

**And that truth is worth the loneliness it takes to hear it.**
