The question of who authored the Song of Songs (also known as the Song of Solomon or Canticles) has generated a wide range of interpretations throughout the history of biblical scholarship. While traditional views strongly attribute the work to King Solomon, modern historical-critical scholars generally argue that the book is anonymously written, with some even proposing female authorship or suggesting that the Holy Spirit is its ultimate author.

Below is a detailed breakdown of the various scholars, their historical or critical contexts, and their specific views on the authorship of the Song.

# Traditional Attribution: King Solomon as the Author
For centuries, the dominant Jewish and Christian consensus held that King Solomon was the historical author of the Song. This view is primarily based on the book's opening superscription: "The Song of Songs, which is Solomon's" (Song 1:1).
- **Origen:** (c. 185–254 AD): One of the earliest and most influential Christian allegorists, Origen firmly endorsed Solomonic authorship. He characterized the book as an epithalamium (a wedding song) written by Solomon in a dramatic style, representing the union of Christ (or the Divine Word) and the Church (or the soul)
- **Augustine of Hippo** (354–430 AD): Augustine maintained that Solomon was the singularauthor of the three canonical books of wisdom (Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, and the Song of Songs). This view was shared by other prominent Latin and Greek fathers, including Ambrose, Gregory of Nyssa, Jerome, and the Venerable Bede
- **Theodoret of Cyr** (c. 393–457 AD): Theodoret vehemently defended Solomonic authorship against literalist critics, arguing that Solomon penned the Song under the influence of the Divine Spirit to outline his mystical wedding with the free people of God.
- **Theodore of Mopsuestia** (c. 350–428 AD): Representing a literalist Antiochene perspective, Theodore believed that Solomon was indeed the author, but argued that he wrote it purely as a secular, erotic poem to defend his marriage to Pharaoh's daughter against popular domestic criticism. 
- **Classical Rabbinic and Medieval Jewish Commentators:** The Targum to the Song of Songs (c. 7th–8th century AD) attributes the book to Solomon, whom it designates as "Solomon, the prophet, the king of Israel" writing under the spirit of prophecy. This was supported by major medieval scholars like Sa'adya Gaon, Rashi (Solomon ben Isaac), and Rashbam (Samuel ben Meir), who read the text as Solomon's divinely inspired allegory portraying the loving, covenantal history between God and Israel. 
- **Franz Delitzsch (19th century):** Delitzsch defended Solomonic authorship, proposing that the Song depicts the literal, historical love relations of Solomon and a rustic Shulamite bride early in his reign before his harem grew excessively large.. 

# Composition in the "Solomonic Era" (Not by Solomon)
Several scholars have argued that while the Song reflects the cultural, artistic, and historical milieu of King Solomon's 10th-century BC court, it was likely written by a contemporary scribe rather than the king himself. 
- **Gillis Gerleman**: Gerleman argued that the Song's intense focus on human beauty, art, and exotic love reflects the period of "Solomonic humanism" or "Solomonic Enlightenment". He suggested that the style was heavily influenced by Egyptian love lyrics of the Ramesside period, which would have been highly accessible to Solomon’s court due to his marriage to an Egyptian princess, though Solomon himself was not the author.
- **M. H. Segal:** Segal assigned the Song to the Solomonic age because the text "breathes the particular atmosphere" of Solomon's worldliness, luxury, and wealth. However, he concluded that it was not written by Solomon, but was instead compiled from oral folk traditions over generations.
- **N. H. Tur-Sinai:** Tur-Sinai suggested that the superscription should be interpreted to mean the songs were composed by poets laureate (hassayyarim) residing in Solomon's court, meaning the poems were written concerning Solomon and his court rather than by him.

# Modern Critical View: An Anonymous Author or Redactor
The majority of modern biblical scholars reject Solomonic authorship. They argue that Solomon's actual historical lifestyle - characterized by 700 wives and 300 concubines (1 Kings 11:3) - directly contradicts the idealized, exclusive, and mutual monogamy celebrated in the Song.
- **Iain Provan:** Provan highlights that Solomon is only ever mentioned in the third person in the Song, and none of the first-person speeches are linked to him. He concludes that the author is completely unknown - likely a postexilic writer standing within the Israelite wisdom tradition who used Solomon as a literary foil. 
- **Roland E. Murphy:** Murphy argues that the traditional Solomonic claim finds no real support in the text. Instead, he suggests the Song is a highly cohesive literary unity crafted by an anonymous postexilic poet (or a very sophisticated editor/redactor) who compiled existing love poems and added the Solomonic superscription to secure its place in wisdom literature. 
- **J. Cheryl Exum:** Exum asserts that the superscription is later than the poem itself, noting that it uses the relative pronoun ’asher while the rest of the book uses the prefix "she". She argues that the attribution to Solomon was the first step in the "religious appropriation" of the book to secure its canonization, but that the actual author remains entirely unknown. 
- **Othmar Keel:** Keel argues that Solomon's name was attached to the Song not because of historical authorship, but because Solomon was revered as the ultimate patron of biblical wisdom. Just as David was the patron of liturgical poetry (Psalms), Solomon was the patron of love and the joys of life. Keel views the final book as a compiled collection of folk love songs.
- **Tremper Longman III:** Longman notes that the Hebrew preposition le in liselomoh can mean "to," "by," "concerning," or "in the tradition of". He argues that the Song is an anonymous anthology of love poems whose title indicates that it is "in the Solomonic/wisdom literary tradition" or "concerns Solomon's legendary wealth and wives" rather than being penned by him. 
- **Iain M. Duguid:** Duguid maintains that the linguistic features of the book (such as Late Biblical Hebrew forms and Persian/Greek loanwords) point to a postexilic date. He suggests the anonymous author intentionally used Solomon as an "anti-hero" to contrast the perfect, faithful devotion of the rustic lovers with the famously unfaithful, excessive lifestyle of the historical King Solomon.
- **Richard S. Hess:** Hess points out that Solomon is never a speaker in the Song, and the anonymous lovers never address him. He concludes that while the themes portray an earlier monarchic environment, the linguistic details suggest a postexilic date for the final anonymous composition.
# The Case for Female Authorship
Because the female voice dominates the Song of Songs to a degree unparalled in almost any other biblical book, several modern scholars have raised the possibility that the author was a woman. 
- **Athalya Brenner:** Brenner calculates that the female character speaks 53% of the text, compared to the male character's 34%. She argues that several poems (such as Song 1:2-6 and 5:10-16) contain a tone, texture, and feminine subjectivity that a male writer could not have successfully imitated, suggesting female authorship at least in the oral stages of the poems.
- **S. D. Goitein:** Goitein proposed that the Song was collected and partially composed by a female court singer in Solomon's court who collected the best of Israelite folk love poetry to entertain the king.
- **André LaCocque:** In his book Romance She Wrote, LaCocque forcefully argues for a female author. He asserts that the book's highly egalitarian gender dynamics and the prominent sexual agency of the female character suggest a woman writer who was subverting the patriarchal norms of her time.
- **Francis Landy:** Landy also notes that the consistent gendering of desire and the dominance of the female protagonist's self-reflection make a female author a highly plausible hypothesis.
- **David J. A. Clines (The Counter-Argument):** Reading "against the grain," Clines strongly rejects female authorship. He argues that the highly assertive, sexually available woman of the Song is actually a male fantasy - a perfect woman fabricated by male writers to satisfy a male public's desire for erotic literature.
- **Agnostic Stance (Longman & Duguid):** Scholars like Longman and Duguid caution that a highly skilled poet of either gender can create exceptionally believable, authentic characters of the opposite sex. Therefore, while female authorship is entirely possible, the gender of the anonymous author remains fundamentally unknowable.

# Divine Authorship: The Holy Spirit
A distinct theological strand of interpretation identifies the Holy Spirit as the true writer of the Song, utilizing Solomon or another wise scribe merely as an inspired instrument.
- **Henry Law:** Law argued that the striking unity of vocabulary, imagery, and thought between the Song and the rest of Scripture (e.g., Christ as the wind, the Church as a vineyard, the calling of Christ to "come") proves unity of divine authorship. For Law, the Holy Spirit is the ultimate writer, and Solomon was the inspired human penman.
- **Didymus the Blind (c. 313–398 AD):** Didymus argued that while a wise man like Solomon may have been physically involved, "actually the Spirit is the author of the divinely inspired Scriptures,"_ breathing the text directly into the human soul.
- **Bernard of Clairvaux (1090–1153 AD):** Bernard, who wrote 86 sermons on the first two chapters of the Song, asserted that the work was composed "not by any human skill but by the artistry of the Spirit," deliberately written in an enigmatic way to entice the faithful to investigate its spiritual depth.
