# Traditional View: The Solomonic Era (10th Century BC)
Historically, Jewish and Christian traditions attributed the book directly to King Solomon. If Solomon was the historical author of the Song, the book must be dated to his reign in the mid-10th century BC (approximately 971–931 BC)
- **Rabbinic Tradition:** Early rabbinic commentators associated the three biblical books attributed to Solomon with three phases of his life, asserting that Solomon wrote the Song of Songs in his youth when a man naturally sings songs.
- **Modern Adherents:** While most critical scholars reject Solomonic authorship due to Solomon's famously non-monogamous lifestyle, some modern conservative scholars, such as Duane Garrett, still defend a 10th-century BC date based on the book's international focus, references to luxury trade, and Solomonic associations.

# Early Iron Age / Northern Dialect Hypothesis (10th to 9th Centuries BC)
Several scholars argue that even if King Solomon did not write the text himself, the core of the Song reflects the early monarchic period of Israel's history.
- **Gillis Gerleman:** Gerleman placed the Song's composition in the 10th century BC, attributing its unique focus on human beauty, art, and nature to the "Solomonic humanism" or "Solomonic Enlightenment" of the era. He argued that active contact with Egyptian love lyrics during the Ramesside period heavily influenced the style of the court, though Solomon was not the author.
- **M. H. Segal:** Segal assigned the Song to the age of Solomon, concluding that its atmosphere of luxury, worldliness, and wealth could only have emerged during Solomon's prosperous and peaceful reign.
- **N. H. Tur-Sinai:** Tur-Sinai dated the songs to the Solomonic era but attributed them to the court poets (hassayyarim) rather than the king himself.
- **Robert Gordis:** Gordis argued that the Song contains a mix of datable units spanning five centuries. He asserted that the mention of the city of Tirzah in Song 6:4 (paired in parallel with Jerusalem) indicates that the core of the poem must predate the early 9th century BC, when King Omri moved the northern capital to Samaria (c. 876 BC).
- **Ian Young, Scott B. Noegel, & Gary A. Rendsburg:** These linguists argue that the book's distinctive language—notably the consistent use of the relative prefix še- rather than the classical ’ăšer - does not prove a late date. Instead, they argue that the Song was written around 900 BC in a distinctive northern dialect of Hebrew, sharing phonetic and grammatical characteristics with Phoenician and early Aramaic.

# Intermediate pre-exilic Period (8th to 6th Centuries BC)
- **Othmar Keel:** Keel proposed that the final collection of the Song was compiled between the 8th and 6th centuries BC. He argued that during this time, Egyptian love poetry was still highly influential. Furthermore, Keel pointed out that the oasis of En-Gedi (mentioned in Song 1:14) was not settled or cultivated until the late 7th century BC, and several of the exotic spice terms point to a late pre-exilic or early exilic date.

# Post-exilic / Persian Period (6th to 4th Centuries BC)
A large number of modern critical scholars favor a post-exilic date, primarily citing the presence of Late Biblical Hebrew syntax, Aramaic vocabulary, and foreign loanwords. 
- **F. W. Dobbs-Allsopp:** Dobbs-Allsopp pointed to the Persian loanword pardes ("orchard" or "park") in Song 4:13 as the most sensitive linguistic barometer for dating. He argued that this and other Persianisms concretely locate the final composition of the poems within the two centuries of Achaemenid (Persian) rule (539–323 BC).
- **Raymond Tournay:** Tournay agreed that the linguistic criteria orient the Song toward the Persian period, citing Persian terms like pardes and karkom ("saffron"), alongside at least seventeen distinct Aramaic words.
- **Iain Provan:** Provan argued that the language of the final text indicates a post-exilic date overall, pointing to the Persian loanword pardes in 4:13 and the book’s use of Late Biblical Hebrew conventions.
- **William Foxwell Albright:** Albright argued for a 5th–4th century BC date for the collection and editing of the Song. He balanced the archaic, second-millennium BC Ugaritic parallels in the imagery with the Persian/Iranian loanwords to conclude that the final editorial work occurred during the Persian era.
- **Richard S. Hess:** Hess suggested that while the themes, agricultural settings, and trade routes depict an earlier monarchic environment, the final composition of the text likely occurred in the post-exilic period, possibly during the era of Ezra and Nehemiah.
# Hellenistic Period (4th to 2nd Centuries BC)
Many contemporary commentators place the final form of the Song in the Hellenistic period, noting potential Greek cultural influences and the loanword ’appiryon (Song 3:9), which is widely believed to derive from the Greek phoreion ("palanquin")
- **Michael V. Fox & J. Cheryl Exum:** Fox and Exum argued that the linguistic profile of the Song exhibits close affinities with Mishnaic Hebrew and Greek-era vocabulary, pointing to a final redaction in the 4th to 2nd centuries BC.
- **Heinrich Graetz:** Graetz famously narrowed the composition of the Song to the late Hellenistic period, specifically placing it between 230 and 218 BC. He argued that the author was a Judean poet who was deeply familiar with Greek language, literature, and customs, and who wrote the Song to counteract Hellenistic moral decline.
- **Hans-Peter Müller:** Müller argued that the Song's final collection and editing was done in the 3rd century BC. He situated the writer among the urban elite of Hellenistic Jerusalem, heavily influenced by the cultured, pleasure-loving Ptolemaic court in Alexandria, Egypt.
- **Carsten Wilke:** Wilke proposed that the Song's structure closely resembles the short lyric Greek idylls that emerged in Alexandria during the first half of the 3rd century BC, suggesting it was written for a sophisticated Judean audience literate in classical Hebrew but integrated into Hellenic culture.
- **Gianni Barbiero:** Barbiero assigned the historical background of the Song to the Hellenistic period, dating it to no earlier than 220 BC.
- **Edmée Kingsmill:** Kingsmill advocated for a very late date, suggesting that the Song of Songs post-dates and alludes to the Book of Sirach (c. 180 BC). She associated the Song's composition with an ascetic, proto-mystical Jewish community in the late 2nd century BC.
- **Giovanni Garbini:** Garbini proposed a highly precise, late Hellenistic date of 69 or 68 BC, linking the book to late Second Temple period political conflicts and literary works.
