There are four main categories : 
1. Allegorical / Spiritual
2. Literal / Natural
3. Political
4. Cultic / Mythological

# Allegorical and Spiritual Purpose : Covenantal and Divine Love
Historically, the dominant Jewish and Christian consensus held that the Song's true purpose was not to celebrate physical intimacy, but to serve as a spiritual allegory depicting the relationship between God and His people. 

## Jewish Covenantal and Mystical Interpretations
- **Rabbi Akiba (c.50-135 AD)** : Akiba famously declared that "the Song of Songs is the Holy of Holies". He championed its canonical status by arguing its purpose was to allegorically depict the covenantal love relationship established between God (the Groom) and Israel (the Bride) at Mount Sinai. To preserve its sanctity, Akiba strictly condemned those who sang the Song profanely in banquet houses. 
- **The Targum of the Song of Songs (c.7th-8th century AD):** The Targum interpreted the book's purpose as a systematic, salvation-historical allegory, mapping the verses to the history of God's love for Israel from the Exodus and Sinai, through the wilderness wanderings, the Temple period, the Exile, and ultimately to the Messianic age. 
- **Rashi (1040-1105):** Rashi held both the literal and allegorical levels together, explaining that the Song's purpose is to depict the voice of Israel as a "woman bound in living widowhood", pining in exile and recalling her youth with her divine husband. 
- **Abraham ibn Ezra (1089-1164):** Ibn Ezra offered a multi-layered interpretation, asserting that on its highest level (pe'amim), the Bride presents the assembly of Israel (knesset yisrael) , and the Song's purpose is to trace the covenantal history from the call of Abraham to the messianic future.  
- **Rashbam (1085–1158):** Rashbam argued that King Solomon wrote the Song under the influence of the Holy Spirit to comfort Israel in exile, showing that their separation from the Holy One is temporary, like a groom temporarily separated from his beloved. 
- **Maimonides (1135–1204) & Gersonides (Levi ben Gershom):** Philosophically minded Jewish scholars argued that the Song's purpose was to depict the yearning of the material or receptive human intellect (represented by the woman) to achieve ultimate union with the Active Intellect, which is God (represented by the man).
- **Don Isaac Abravanel (d. 1508):** Abravanel suggested the Song’s purpose was to celebrate the love between King Solomon and His "bride," who represents personified Wisdom.
- **Edmée Kingsmill:** In modern scholarship, Kingsmill defends the traditional mystical interpretation, arguing that the Song is a unified allegory representing the love of God for His creation, the Temple, and His chosen people, portraying an eschatological paradise that reverses the prophetic theme of Israel's betrayal.

## Christian Christological and Ecclesiological Interpretations
- **Origen of Alexandria (c. 185–254 AD):** Origen, who wrote a monumental ten-volume commentary, argued that the Song is an epithalamium (wedding song) written by Solomon in the form of a drama. He maintained that its true, spiritual purpose is to depict the mystical union of Christ (the Bridegroom) and the Church (the Bride), or Christ and the individual soul. Origen viewed it as an advanced "contemplative discipline" instructing the soul in divine love. 
- **Hippolytus of Rome (c. 200 AD):** The earliest Christian writer to comment on the Song, Hippolytus used the text to affirm asceticism and interpreted it as a salvation-historical prophecy showing the transition from the old covenant to the new, with the Church of the Gentiles replacing Israel as the object of God's love.
- **Gregory of Nyssa (c. 335–394 AD):** Gregory argued that the Bride represents the individual human soul and that the Song’s purpose is to outline a philosophy showing the soul's ascent to spiritual perfection and union with God. 
- **Theodoret of Cyr (c. 393–457 AD):** Theodoret vehemently defended the spiritual nature of the Song, explaining that its purpose is to outline a mystical wedding, portraying Christ's love for a redeemed people and revealing the "holiest of holy mysteries of divine loving-kindness".
- **Augustine of Hippo (354–430 AD):** Augustine maintained that the Song represents the "spiritual joy of saintly souls" at the mystical nuptials of Christ and His Church, hidden under an allegorical veil to make the discovery more delightful.
- **Bernard of Clairvaux (1090–1153):** Bernard wrote eighty-six sermons on the first two chapters, asserting that the Song's purpose is to depict the intimate, contemplative union of the soul and God, reflecting the "alternating visits of the Word".
- **Henry Law:** Law argued that the Holy Spirit used Solomon to write the Song as a "chaste prelude to the chorus of the white-robed multitude", establishing the unity of Christ and the Church.

## The Literal and Natural Purpose: Celebrating Human Love, Sexuality, and Marriage
Modern critical scholars generally reject allegorical readings, arguing that the Song has a deeply humanistic, non-allegorical purpose: to celebrate the goodness of physical love, romantic desire, and mutual sexuality within creation. 
- **Theodore of Mopsuestia (c. 350–428 AD):** The sole dissenting voice in the patristic era, Theodore rejected allegory and argued that the Song was written strictly as a secular love poem sung by Solomon to celebrate and defend his marriage to Pharaoh's daughter against popular domestic criticism.
- **Brevard S. Childs:** Childs argued that the Song must be understood as wisdom literature whose purpose is "wisdom's reflection on the joyful and mysterious nature of love between a man and a woman within the institution of marriage".
- **Jean-Paul Audet:** Audet linked the Song's origin to ancient popular rites of betrothal, suggesting that postexilic wisdom sages compiled these love lyrics as a powerful affirmation of human sexual love as a divine blessing compatible with marital fidelity.
- **Othmar Keel:** Keel argues that the Song was not originally written as an allegory, but rather as an open, non-mythological, and non-cultic celebration of God-given sexual love. 
- **Richard S. Hess:** Hess asserts that the Song's purpose is to "endorse sex and celebrate it beyond all expectation", avoiding both the cheapening of sex into promiscuity and the locking away of this gift through asceticism. 
- **Roland E. Murphy:** Murphy argues the Song is a "poetic exploration of human sexual love" that models an important, divinely sanctioned dimension of human existence, inviting readers to appreciate the qualities of joy, sensual intimacy, and reciprocal longing.
- **Paul J. Griffiths:** Griffiths advocates a figural approach where the human literal reality of the text is not dissolved, noting that the Song's depiction of sexual desire and love figures both the Lord's love for us and our reciprocal love for Him, showing that human love participates in divine love.
## Feminist and Gynocentric Interpretations: Restoring Sexual Agency
- **Phyllis Trible:** In her pioneering work, Trible argued that the Song's purpose is to celebrate "love for the sake of love" in a non-sexist world. She views it as a "redemptive counter-text" that portrays a mutuality of the sexes free of male dominance or female subordination, effectively returning the human couple to a pre-Fall, egalitarian Eden
- **Carol Meyers:** Meyers highlights that a "gynocentric mode predominates" in the Song, giving a powerful voice to female initiative, desire, and agency in contrast to the heavily male-dominated perspective of the rest of the Bible. 
- **J. Cheryl Exum:** Exum views the Song's purpose as an exploration of erotic love and sexual desire from both a woman's and a man's point of view, validating female sexual agency and showing that romantic love transforms how lovers perceive the world. 
- **Havilah Dharamraj:** Dharamraj argues that the Song serves as a bold, canonical "other half of a phone conversation". While law and narrative in the Old Testament assume women lack sexual agency, the Song presents a female protagonist who is completely uninhibited in choosing with whom, where, and when she will lie.

### An Anti-Solomonic Foil: An Ethical Purpose
- **Iain Provan:** Provan adopts a three-character dramatic reading to argue that the Song has a deeply ethical purpose: it uses King Solomon as an "anti-hero" to contrast the mutual, committed, and freely chosen love of the rustic couple with the coercive, dominant, and transactional power of Solomon’s palace.
- **Iain M. Duguid:** Duguid maintains that the Song is an idealized wisdom piece whose purpose is to contrast the exclusive, wholehearted, and lifelong devotion of the rustic couple with the notoriously unfaithful, commercial, and polygamous "Solomonic model" of relationships.
## The Political Purpose: An Encomium of State Peace
- **Martin Luther (1483–1546):** Rejecting both Origen's mystical view and Theodore's erotic view, Luther argued that the Song's purpose is entirely political. He maintained that Solomon wrote it as an "encomium of the political order" to thank God for the highest blessing of external peace and to serve as an example of civic gratitude.
## The Cultic/Mythological Purpose: Rites of Sacred Marriage
- **Theophile J. Meek & Samuel Noah Kramer:** Emerging in the early twentieth century, these scholars argued that the Song's original pre-history was a cultic liturgy of Canaanite fertility rites or Mesopotamian sacred marriage (hieros gamos) rituals. Its purpose was to liturgically celebrate the union of divine figures (such as Tammuz/Dumuzi and Ishtar/Inanna) to magically stimulate the fertility of the earth and the renewal of the seasons.
